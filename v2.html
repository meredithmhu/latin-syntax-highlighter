The Toothpaste Poem

Apuleius — Apologia, Chapter 6
(3)

Calpurniane, salve properis versibus.

Greetings, Calpurnius with these swift verses.

(4)

Misi, ut petisti, munditias dentium

I sent, as you asked, to you, the cleaning supplies for your teeth.

(5)

nitelas oris ex Arabicis frugibus

Whitening agents of the mouth — from Arabic plants.

(6)

tenuem, candificum, nobilem pulvisculum,

Delicate, glowing, shining glittery dust powder

(7)

complanatorem tumidulae gingivulae,

The 'leveler' of swollen gums,

(8)

converritorem pridianae reliquiae,

The conqueror/street sweeper of yesterday's leftovers

(9)

ne qua visatur tetra labes sordium,

so that: no offensive stain of dirt may be seen

(10)

restrictis forte si labellis riseris.

If by any chance you happen to laugh (with your lips pulled back).

(11)

Quaeso, quid habent isti versus re aut verbo pudendum, quid omnino quod philosophus suum nolit videri?

I ask (oh please), what do these verses have in subject or language that should make me ashamed; what, at all, do they have, which a philosopher would not want to seem his own?
